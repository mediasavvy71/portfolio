Case Study 06 · Identity & Access Management

A Role Framework Built to Outgrow Itself

Redesigning how a national wealth management platform defines, assigns, and enforces user roles — so advisory firms can describe their own people in plain language while the system underneath stays governed and safe to scale to institutions far larger than it was built for.

Role

Overview

LPL Financial supports thousands of independent advisory firms and institutions, each organizing its people differently. The platform's identity systems, however, were built around a much narrower idea of "who a user is" — and as firms grew larger and more complex, that mismatch turned into a scaling problem the business could no longer route around.

I led UX for the Access workstream within Personalization, designing the framework that lets firms label and structure their people in their own terms, while a layered system underneath translates those labels into governed, auditable platform access.

The Problem

The business case going in was blunt: existing systems were being stretched past their design intent, and the workaround culture built up around them had reached its limit.

From the program's founding brief: "LPL has 'gamed' our internal systems to meet business needs, but scale is a problem. The current approach is reaching a breaking point and we are running out of rep IDs to continue to operate within the existing construct."

Three forces were converging:

  • Growing complexity of demand. Larger, more sophisticated advisory firms and institutions needed to express nuanced internal structures — job titles, branch hierarchies, regional roles — that the platform had no vocabulary for.
  • Systems used past their intent. Tools like ComReg were never designed to carry this weight, and were being forced into a role they couldn't structurally support.
  • A finite workaround. Teams had been manually creating duplicate rep IDs to fake the flexibility firms needed — an approach that doesn't scale, and one the org was running out of runway on.

My job was to design a system where firms could define how they see themselves — their titles, their branches — without every new role or edge case requiring a code change on our end.

Design Approach

Rather than treat role definition, role assignment, and access enforcement as one blended workflow, I split responsibility across three layers so that flexibility for the firm never came at the cost of control for the platform:

Define — IDWorks

The source of truth for role structure and rules. This is where the underlying taxonomy and validation logic live, so nothing downstream can assign an unsafe or unsupported role.

Assign — Business Configurator

The interface I designed for firms and internal Personalization Ops to label and assign roles — "Jr. Advisor," "5th Street Branch Officer" — in plain business language, without needing to understand the system underneath.

Enforce — Wardens / FR

The authorization layer (ForgeRock) that validates every change against IDWorks' rules and applies real system access automatically via JML.

Translate — IdentityWorks

Takes the plain-language label plus basic user data and determines which governed access role the person should actually receive.

The Access framework: definition, assignment, translation, and enforcement are deliberately separated across four systems.

Key Design Decisions

1. Let firms speak their own language, not ours

The core insight I designed around: firms don't think in terms of "Associate Type" or system role codes — they think in terms of "Jr. Advisor" or "5th Street Branch Officer." Business Configurator gives firms a plain-language labeling layer, while IdentityWorks quietly translates those labels into governed system roles behind the scenes. This kept the interface human without weakening the underlying access model.

2. Separate "who defines a role" from "who assigns it"

One of the more important architectural decisions I pushed for was keeping role definition (IDWorks) and role assignment (Business Configurator) as distinct layers with distinct owners. Business Configurator was never meant to let anyone invent a new role on the fly — it lets Ops and firms manage who holds which already-governed role, with every change validated against IDWorks' rules and enforced through Wardens/FR. That separation is what makes the system safe to eventually open up to larger external clients.

3. Design for roles that don't exist yet

Because client organizations evolve faster than any static role list, I designed the framework to accommodate emerging, unforeseen roles rather than a fixed enumeration — extending LPL's existing roles framework instead of replacing it, so it could flex with client structure changes without a re-architecture each time.

Constraints I designed within

  • Legacy systems of record. IDWorks and the underlying authorization layer were non-negotiable — any new interface had to sit on top of existing rules, not replace them.
  • Multiple affiliation models. The framework needed to work across every way a firm or institution can be affiliated with LPL, not a single relationship type.
  • Internal operators as primary users, firms as the long-term audience. Early usage centers on internal Personalization Ops assigning roles on firms' behalf; the design had to work today for an internal power-user workflow while remaining simple enough to hand directly to external clients later.

Status & Next Steps

In-flight program

This workstream is actively rolling out in phases rather than shipped end-to-end. The near-term focus is externalizing Business Configurator so firms — not just internal Ops — can manage their own roles directly, which is the point at which this framework's flexibility starts paying off at scale.

Reflection

The hardest part of this project wasn't the interface — it was resisting the urge to solve it as a UI problem before it was solved as a systems problem. The design that ended up working is really an information architecture decision disguised as a form: deciding what belongs to IDWorks versus Business Configurator versus Wardens/FR determined almost everything about how simple the resulting screens could be. Getting that boundary right meant firms get a genuinely simple, plain-language experience, while the platform underneath stays governed and safe to scale.
