Letting Every Firm Own Its Brand

Designing a centralized branding layer inside the Business Configurator so advisory firms can control how their identity shows up across every client touchpoint — without an engineering ticket for every logo or letterhead change.

Overview

LPL Financial supports thousands of independent advisory firms and institutions, each with its own visual identity and its own expectations for how that identity should appear in client-facing materials. Before this work, branding requests — a new logo on a statement, a firm's colors on a report — routed through engineering one at a time, which meant firms waited on a queue for something as basic as looking like themselves.

I led the design of Flexible Branding, a capability inside the Business Configurator that lets firms manage their own branding assets and attributes directly, and see that identity carried consistently across reports, statements, letters, and the product's own interface.

The Problem

Flexible Branding sits inside the larger Personalization program, which exists because LPL's advisor and institutional clients were outgrowing systems that assumed one brand, one look, one workflow. Branding was one of the clearest symptoms:

  • No self-service path. Firms had no way to manage their own brand presentation — every change was a request into an engineering queue, regardless of how small.
  • Inconsistency across outputs. Reports, letters, statements, and in-product screens were each built and branded somewhat independently, so a firm's identity could look different depending on which output they were looking at.
  • No digital/print parity. Firms operate across both digital and print touchpoints with their end clients; the platform had no unified way to guarantee those matched.

The goal wasn't a branding "feature" — it was a control surface that firms could trust to represent them correctly, everywhere, without our team in the loop for routine changes.

Design Approach

I designed Flexible Branding as a single, centrally managed configuration layer rather than a set of one-off branding fields scattered across each output. The core decision: branding assets and attributes are defined once, in one place, and every downstream output reads from that same source.

Define once

Firms manage branding assets and attributes centrally in the Business Configurator — logos, colors, and identity attributes live in a single governed location rather than duplicated per output.

Apply everywhere

That definition propagates automatically into downstream outputs: reports, letters, statements, and elements within the product's own user interface.

Digital + print parity

The same configuration governs both digital and print touchpoints, so a firm's brand reads as one cohesive identity regardless of the channel a client sees it through.

Self-service, not a ticket

Firms — not engineering — own the update. A brand change becomes a configuration change, not a development cycle.

One governed configuration in Business Configurator drives every branded output, digital or print.

Key Design Decisions

1. Treat branding as configuration, not code

The single most important framing decision was refusing to let "branding" be defined per-output. Instead of a logo field on the reports team's roadmap and a separate one on the statements team's roadmap, Flexible Branding lives in one place and every output is a consumer of that same source of truth. That's what makes it self-service instead of a recurring backlog item.

2. Design for the outputs firms actually see

I scoped the surface around the real set of client-facing touchpoints — reports, letters, statements, and in-product UI elements — rather than an abstract "branding module." Firms don't think about branding in the abstract; they think about what their client is holding or looking at, so the configuration model mirrors that.

3. Give digital and print equal weight

It would have been easier to design for digital alone and treat print as an afterthought. Because firms' clients experience both, I designed the configuration to apply identically across channels, so a firm never has to reconcile two different "sources of truth" for their own brand.

Constraints I designed within

  • No single owner of "brand" before this. Logos, colors, and identity attributes existed in fragments across teams and outputs; part of the design work was defining what "centrally managed" should even mean operationally.
  • Every affiliation model, not just one. The configuration needed to work regardless of how a firm or institution is affiliated with LPL, which ruled out shortcuts specific to any single relationship type.
  • Shared foundation with the Access framework. Flexible Branding lives in the same Business Configurator surface used for role assignment (see Case Study 06), so the two had to share a coherent information architecture rather than compete for space.

Status & Next Steps

In-flight program

Flexible Branding is rolling out as part of the broader Personalization program rather than shipping as a single release. The near-term focus is extending self-service configuration further out to firms directly, reducing the volume of branding requests that still route through internal teams today.

Reflection

What made this project a design problem rather than a settings-page exercise was resisting the instinct to bolt a "branding tab" onto each existing output. The real work was recognizing that reports, letters, statements, and UI were all downstream of the same unmet need — a firm wanting to look like itself — and designing one governed source that all of them could draw from. That decision is what turns "add our logo" from an engineering request into something a firm can just do.
