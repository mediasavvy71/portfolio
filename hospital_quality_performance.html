1Context & Problem Space

Quality data collected everywhere — actionable nowhere

My Contributions

  • Led cross-functional discovery across clinical SMEs, quality analysts, and medical directors — mapping a fragmented data ecosystem spanning 5 reporting formats and organizational levels
  • Created qualitative personas grounded in observed workflows, needs, and failure modes of quality data roles
  • Synthesized research into an information architecture that unified measure categories, organizational hierarchies, and KPI comparisons into a single experience
  • Produced user flows, storyboards, wireframes, and high-fidelity mockups; championed design patterns for data-dense clinical environments
  • Advocated for accessibility and inclusive design in chart legibility, contrast ratios, and information hierarchy for time-pressured clinical users

Healthcare organizations are assessed on quality measures across dozens of clinical categories — mortality, safety of care, readmissions, patient experience. The data exists, but it's collected and stored across multiple systems in multiple formats. By the time it reaches the people who need to act on it, it's often six months old.

Quality analysts, medical directors, and department heads had no unified view of their performance. They couldn't identify where they were falling short, compare themselves against peers, or know which corrective actions to prioritize — in time to make a difference.

Fragmented Data Ecosystem

Quality measures were collected, stored, and reported across five disconnected systems. No single view of organizational performance existed across measure categories or facility levels.

Stale, Unusable Reporting

Reports were delivered 4–6 months after the measurement period — by which time the data reflected conditions too old to act on. Users described the reports as "already historical" on arrival.

No Path to Corrective Action

Even when users could identify a performance gap, the system provided no guidance on what to do next. Corrective action planning was done entirely outside the platform, in spreadsheets and email.

"The data is there — we just can't see it until it's too late to do anything about it."

2Discovery & Systems Mapping

A cross-functional research effort to map the full quality data ecosystem

Our UX team conducted formative research by interviewing and observing primary stakeholders across their daily workflows. I collaborated with Clinical Subject Matter Experts to map the quality data process end-to-end — understanding how a quality analyst actually does their job, what data they access, where they get stuck, and what decisions they need to make.

A critical finding emerged early: organization size fundamentally shapes the quality analyst role. In smaller facilities, one analyst handles every phase of the quality cycle — from pulling raw measures to presenting board-level reports. In larger systems, these responsibilities are distributed across specialized roles. This insight directly drove our persona architecture and the role-based navigation system we designed.

🏥

Org Size Shapes Everything

Smaller organizations have one analyst handling the full quality cycle. Larger systems distribute work across 4–6 specialized roles. A single UX pattern couldn't serve both without role-based customization.

📊

Volume Overwhelm is Universal

Regardless of org size, every quality analyst cited data volume as their primary challenge. They spend more time assembling data than analyzing it — the system should do the assembly.

🔗

Five Systems, No Single Source

Quality data flowed through CMS, internal QI platforms, departmental spreadsheets, state reporting tools, and EHR exports. None of them talked to each other.

🎯

Action is the Missing Link

Users could eventually identify a performance gap — but the path from "we're underperforming on readmissions" to "here's what we do about it" was entirely outside the system.

3Design Process

From ecosystem map to unified design system for quality intelligence

Research revealed that the problem wasn't a missing chart — it was a missing architecture. Quality data existed in silos because no one had designed an information model that connected measure categories, organizational levels, and time dimensions into a single navigable structure.

I designed a role-driven experience where each persona enters at the right level of the data hierarchy and can drill down or escalate without leaving the platform. The recommendation system surfaces corrective actions inline — closing the loop that had always required a separate workflow outside the system.

Design System Contributions
→Established chart component patterns for data-dense clinical contexts — optimized for contrast ratios and legibility under time pressure
→Designed a reusable measure category card component used across 4 clinical domains (Mortality, Safety, Readmissions, Patient Experience)
→Created accessibility-first data visualization guidelines — WCAG AA compliant color system for performance indicators (good / watch / critical)
→Defined progressive disclosure patterns for org-to-facility-to-measure drill-down navigation

4Validate

Closing the loop with clinical SMEs and stakeholder feedback

After producing high-fidelity mockups, we returned to the clinical SMEs and quality analysts who participated in discovery to validate key design decisions. Sessions focused on three questions: Does the hierarchy match how you actually think about your data? Can you find a performance gap within the first two interactions? Does the corrective action recommendation feel trustworthy and actionable?

Feedback was used to refine the drill-down navigation pattern, simplify the measure category overview cards, and strengthen the contrast between "on target," "watch," and "critical" performance states — a direct response to accessibility feedback from users working on smaller monitors in clinical environments.

What we validated
✓Org → Facility → Measure hierarchy matched mental model of quality analysts
✓Performance gap identifiable within 2 clicks from dashboard landing
✓Corrective action recommendations felt relevant and trustworthy to clinical directors
✓Color + icon system legible on clinical workstation monitors at standard viewing distance
What we refined
↻Simplified measure category card to show rank order + trend instead of raw score
↻Strengthened contrast ratio on performance states — increased to 5.2:1 for critical indicators
↻Added icon reinforcement to performance states so color is never the sole indicator
5Outcomes & Impact

From siloed data to a platform the business could take to market

🔗

Five Systems Unified Into One Experience

The information architecture and role-driven navigation model provided a single entry point into quality data that had previously lived across CMS portals, QI platforms, state systems, EHR exports, and departmental spreadsheets.

⚡

Near Real-Time KPI Visibility

Replacing 6-month-old static reports with a near real-time dashboard gave quality analysts and clinical directors a fundamentally different relationship with their performance data — actionable instead of historical.

🎯

Corrective Action Integrated Into the Platform

For the first time, the path from identifying a performance gap to initiating a corrective action plan existed within a single experience — eliminating the spreadsheet and email-based workaround that had been standard practice.

♿

Accessible Performance Indicator System

The WCAG AA-compliant color + icon system for performance states was adopted as a standard for data visualization across the product suite — ensuring legibility for colorblind users and those working in variable lighting conditions.

📈

Business Confidence to Go to Market

The business team advanced the product to market following design completion, with the client requesting continued engagement to expand the platform's scope — a direct signal of stakeholder confidence in the experience design.
