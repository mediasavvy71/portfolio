Rationalizing the Procure-to-Pay ecosystem across tools, workflows, and stakeholder groups — surfacing where experience breakdowns were driving cost, risk, and lost decision quality.

Problem

An ecosystem that evolved faster than it was ever designed

Premier's Procure-to-Pay environment grew organically over years — leaving no shared, structured understanding of how the pieces fit together.

Fragmented toolsAcross procurement, contracting, and supply chain workflows
Inconsistent experienceQuality varied sharply across clinicians, procurement, finance, suppliers
Low visibilityNo end-to-end view of operational flow
Redundant capabilityOverlapping systems with unclear ownership
Approach

Four layers, moving from discovery to investment-ready opportunity

Structured as a systems-level engagement — not a feature redesign.

1

Ecosystem Discovery & Alignment

Stakeholder interviews across product, operations, clinical and finance; audit of fragmented journey documentation; full system inventory; review of business objectives and cost drivers.

Output → Ecosystem inventory & relationship map
2

Ecosystem Mapping — System-Level View

Mapped user roles, the request → approve → procure → pay workflow, supporting systems at each step, and every handoff between them — surfacing duplicated work and manual "bridging" behavior.

Output → Multi-role, multi-system ecosystem map
3

Capability Heat Map

Scored every capability on coverage, consistency, and criticality — exposing high-cost / low-maturity workflows, over-invested overlapping tools, and orphan capabilities with no owner.

Output → Investment + UX maturity heat map
4

Opportunity Definition — OST + Synthesis

Built an Opportunity Solution Tree connecting business outcomes to user needs to system gaps, converting research into a structured, prioritized set of opportunity areas.

Output → Opportunity Solution Tree aligned to business outcomes

Synthesis

Key Insights

01

The problem was never the UI

Most UX issues were downstream of structural duplication and unclear system ownership — not interface quality.

02

Users were the integration layer

Procurement and finance staff manually reconciled systems that should have been connected from the start.

03

Investment decisions lacked experience visibility

Teams optimized within silos rather than across the end-to-end journey, missing the highest-leverage fixes.

04

Capability overlap was hiding real cost

Multiple systems served the same function at different maturity and adoption levels — with no shared view of the redundancy.
